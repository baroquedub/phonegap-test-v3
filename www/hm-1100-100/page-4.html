Field and Desk Research

Another important distinction in market research is between field research (collecting original or primary data) and desk research.

Field research consists of conducting interviews, surveys or observations. Field research can produce very specific data but is expensive and time consuming.

Desk research uses data that has already been obtained, analysed and used for some other purpose – sometimes known as secondary information. It is relatively quick and inexpensive but the data itself can be out of date and may not be not specific to the user's needs. Potential sources are shown in the table below

The aim here is to measure and evaluate customers' reactions to changes in the 'marketing mix' (in the product, the way it is promoted etc). Examples include test markets (launching a product to a particular group of people) or pilot trials in particular areas.

A data gathering approach in which information is collected without questions being asked. Involves watching people behave, noting and analysing their reactions. It is useful to find out how people buy products, where they shop, what appears to interest them. Common methods are traffic audits, TV and radio panels and retail store audits.

This is the key research method to obtain information from large samples. The issues involved with conducting surveys are very similar to those of clinical trials – finding a representative population, ensuring that survey is conducted in a consistent manner, asking the right questions, processing and interpreting the data. There are various ways of conducting market research surveys - face to face, phone, postal, online- and each has its own merits and drawbacks.

Strengths and weaknesses of some methods of information gathering for marketing research)
Face to face interviews
Strengths Weaknesses
  • Interviewer can prompt the respondent
  • Can see respondents reaction
  • Respondents have more time to think
  • Detailed responses
  • Can get opinions
  • Respondents give full attention
  • Flexible
  • Allows probing
  • Trust can be built up to get more reliable data
  • High cost
  • Time consuming
  • Risk of bias
  • Requires interview skills
Focus groups
Used for qualitative information gathering. Usually consist of 8-10 respondents and a moderator. The moderator introduces topics and guides conversation with the aim of seeking opinions and discovering attitudes. The validity of the output will often depend on how well the composition of the group reflects the target audience.
Strengths Weaknesses
  • Quick
  • Range of attitudes
  • Detailed qualitative information obtained
  • Flow of discussion encourages  ideas and participation
  • Trust can be built up to get more reliable data
  • Need to build rapport
  • Discussions must stay focused
  • Needs good control by the moderator
  • Some members may be inhibited
  • Can be time consuming and expensive to set up
Panels
Panels represent a 'continuous' form or research, rather than ad hoc research which is set up specifically to address a need. Panels consist of a representative sample of individuals who are questioned regularly – they are useful in assessing shifts of attitude and opinions over time. Panel members are usually induced to be permanently available by means of small payments.
Strengths Weaknesses
  • A good trend indicator
  • Useful for analysing changes
  • Sample may be intrinsically biased or unrepresentative
  • Panellists may adopt uncharacteristic behaviour during panel sessions
  • Panels have to be replaced periodically
  • When novelty wears off members become less co-operative
